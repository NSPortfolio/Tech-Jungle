Disposing Unwanted and Old Electronics Harms Our Environment


E-Waste


According to the Platform for Accelerating the Circular Economy and the UN E-Waste Coalition, we produce almost 50 tons of electronic waste or e-waste worth over $62.5 billion every year.


Less than 20% of e-waste is formally recycled to reuse heavy metals or other components, and 80% ends up in landfills or informally recycled.


If our e-waste curve continues as predicted, we could find 120 tons of it being thrown out yearly by 2050.


Why is e-waste a problem?

Our Atmosphere

Electronic waste that ends up in landfills or informal recycling areas releases toxic chemicals including lead into the surrounding atmosphere. This process negatively affects both the neurological and overall health of animals and us humans.

Our Rainforests

Toxins like heavy metals and flame retardants that leach from e-waste contaminate soil as well. These contaminants are taken up by crops and rainforests affecting plant growth and processes. Eventually, these crops and plants reach consumer mouths, potentially causing health concerns. In particular, the mining of cobalt, one of the primary components in cell phones, in the Congo Basin increases the hunting and killing of endangered gorillas and apes. Recycling or reusing a cell phone can save a gorilla.

Our Water

Toxins can seep into groundwater or flow into rivers and streams. This results in contaminated drinking water containing potentially lead, arsenic, and mercury and toxification in water habitats and food sources.



What can we do?

Sell or Recycle

According to the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), we toss out over 416,000 cell phones and 142,000 computers. Instead of trashing unwanted and broken electronics, sell them to those who may find them valuable.
